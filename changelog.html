September 28, 2025

The Navbar Nightmare Chronicles
Outcome: Bug fixed, code runs, confidence restored (temporarily)

Dearest Code Warriors, Reluctant Debuggers, and Fellow Sufferers of Unexpected Site Shenanigans,
Prepare yourselves to dive headfirst into a tale of noble intentions, sneaky bugs, and the tragic downfall of a humble navbar.

Remember a while ago when I added a navbar to my website? I had been neck-deep in web design tutorials for hours. Somewhere between “this’ll be quick” and “why is it 2 a.m.,” I thought, “You know what would make my site pop? A sleek little navbar.”

So I built one. It looked good. It felt good. It worked. At least, for a while.

Fast-forward a few weeks. I got inspired again and decided to add page animations.

Did I stop and think about how those animations would interact with the navbar? Of course not. I was in "just one more little feature" mode.

It was supposed to be simple. Chill. A sprinkle of motion to make the site feel alive. Instead, the universe decided to give me a lesson in humility.

A few weeks later, I'm sitting at school, casually pulling up my site, ready to bask in my coding glory. I click on the navbar links, expecting to be whisked away to my pages like a boss. Instead, I get a lovely little message that says, "Moved to line one."

I stared at the screen. Hard. Like maybe if I stared long enough, it would go away or explain itself. It didn’t. It just sat there, mocking me. Like, "You thought you were in control? Cute."

Was it a bug? A feature? A CSS demon? A forgotten line of JavaScript from 3 a.m. brain fog? I had no clue. All I knew was that my navbar was cursed, and I was about to spiral.

Now, I had homework. I had a life. I couldn’t deal with it right then. So I did what every developer tells themselves: "I’ll fix it on Saturday."

Friday: False Hope

I usually start my weekend coding a little early. So Friday after school, I sat down and started poking around.

Now, I actually had solid plans for Saturday. I wasn’t just going to fix a bug. I wanted to:

  • Add a new project to the site
  • Expand the project list so each one had its own heading
  • Write short descriptions for each project
  • Add a quick explanation in the "About Me" section because right now it just jumps straight into my info like a Wikipedia article
  • Do the same for the Projects section to give some context before throwing links at people

But no. This bug had other ideas.

What followed was three straight hours of total failure. What I tried:

  • Scroll-top fixes
  • Scroll-top combined with fixed navbar
  • Random divs with 200px height before each section
  • CSS min-height tweaks
  • Even JavaScript that manually calculated and adjusted scroll positions

Absolutely nothing worked.

At one point, I seriously thought my code was haunted. I stared at it like it had betrayed me. Then I did what any reasonable person would do. I shut the laptop, rage-quit GitHub, and told myself, "If this isn’t fixed by tomorrow, the navbar is dead."

Saturday: The Reckoning

I had real plans. I was supposed to be productive. But instead of working on all the cool features I had lined up, I was back in the trenches, fighting the same stupid bug.

Three more hours of Googling, testing, breaking things, unbreaking them, only to break them again. Still no fix. Still the same "Moved to line one" nonsense.

At some point, I realized something important.

This navbar was not worth it.

It had officially crossed the line from "fun little feature" to "enemy of the state." So I made a decision.

I deleted it.

Gone. Wiped off the face of the repo. Good riddance.

The Sidebar Era Begins

I replaced the navbar with a sidebar. And honestly? That move was elite.

Let me explain why:

  1. More space. It doesn't cram everything into one thin strip across the top.
  2. Way better on mobile. No weird collapsing or stretching. Just clean toggles.
  3. Easier navigation. I could fit more links without making it look like a cluttered mess.
  4. It looks cooler. Sidebars just have that modern, sleek vibe. Navbars are fine, but they're the vanilla ice cream of layout design.
  5. More flexible. I can put the sidebar on the left or right. Navbars are stuck at the top like a stubborn ceiling fan.
  6. Better user experience. People expect sidebars these days. It makes the site feel more intuitive.

So yeah. I switched. And life was good.

No regrets.

Well, almost.

Because even after switching to the sidebar, the "Moved to line one" message still showed up. I couldn't believe it. I had completely deleted the navbar, and yet the chaos remained.

At that point, I started thinking maybe my site was cursed.

But after digging deeper, I finally found the culprit. It had nothing to do with the navbar at all. It was some scroll behavior in the JavaScript that didn’t account for a fixed element at the top of the page. One small script change later, everything worked.

Could I have saved the navbar?

Maybe.

Would I go back and keep it?

Not a chance.

It cost me a weekend, hijacked my entire to-do list, and burned through my motivation like it was kindling. But in the end, I still walked away with a working site and a design that feels way better than before.

The sidebar works. The bug is dead. And I can finally move on.

What Did I Learn?

  • Just because something looks good in a tutorial doesn’t mean it’s going to work on your site
  • Always test how different features interact, especially animations
  • Bugs don’t care about your calendar
  • Sometimes you have to scrap the idea, not just patch it
  • Bugs will always eat your weekend if you let them
  • Writing about your pain makes it slightly more tolerable
  • And finally, some bugs are just there to remind you that you're not as in control as you think

This bug was brutal. I’ve dealt with annoying collapsible menus. I’ve survived layout-breaking CSS. But this one? This one had a personal vendetta.

Still, the site works now. The sidebar looks better. The bug is gone. And even though I lost a whole weekend, I gained a solid lesson in what not to do.

Next time, maybe I’ll plan ahead. Or maybe I’ll wing it again and just hope I don’t end up writing another one of these.

Either way, I’ll be laughing about it by Sunday night. Probably.

So yeah, the navbar's gone. The bug's gone. My weekend? Also gone.

But hey, the site's working, the sidebar actually makes things better, and I can finally stop questioning my life choices—at least when it comes to scroll behavior.

Was it annoying? Absolutely. Was it avoidable? Probably. Would I do it all the same way again? …Don’t ask me that right now.

For now, I'm just glad it's over. Let the sidebar era begin.
